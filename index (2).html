Directions for the Teacher

📋
About this lesson: Students will watch a short science video that challenges a common belief — that boredom is always bad. They will develop vocabulary, practice auxiliary verbs, build sentences, and discuss the topic in pairs or groups.

Hand out the lesson. Students read the Background section silently or aloud.
Step 2
Pre-teach vocabulary. Students complete the Vocabulary table before watching.

Step 4

Background

🌐
Veritasium is a popular science YouTube channel hosted by Derek Muller, a science communicator from Australia. The name comes from the Latin word for "truth." The channel explores ideas in physics, biology, mathematics, and everyday life — using experiments, interviews, and storytelling to challenge what we think we know.

In this video, Derek asks a surprising question: Is boredom actually good for us? Most people try to avoid boredom by reaching for their phones. But scientific research suggests that when we allow ourselves to feel bored, our brains do something remarkable — they become more creative, more reflective, and better at planning for the future. Derek explores studies and ideas that show boredom is not wasted time. In fact, it might be essential time.

Grammar Focus: Auxiliary Verbs

🔧

What are auxiliary verbs?

Auxiliary verbs (also called "helping verbs") work together with a main verb to express meaning. In the video, Derek uses auxiliary verbs constantly to talk about possibility, ability, obligation, and habits.

can / could · will / would · should / must · may / might · have to · be going to
  • can → ability: "Boredom can make you more creative."
  • should → advice: "You should allow yourself to feel bored."
  • might → possibility: "You might have a great idea if you stop scrolling."
  • will → future result: "If you're always distracted, you will rarely think about your goals."
  • have to → necessity: "We have to resist the urge to check our phones."
  • could → past ability / suggestion: "She could not resist the distraction."

Exercise A — Choose the Correct Auxiliary Verb

Circle the best option to complete each sentence.

  1. Boredom ________ (can / must) actually help you become more creative, according to research.
  2. You ________ (should / will) try sitting quietly without your phone for at least ten minutes a day.
  3. If you always use your phone when you're bored, you ________ (might / have to) miss important thinking time.
  4. Scientists say the brain ________ (could / will) start planning for the future when it has nothing else to do.
  5. We ________ (must / can) not ignore the importance of free, unstructured time for our mental health.
  6. She ________ (would / could) not remember the last time she was truly bored without her phone.
  7. People ________ (may / should) experience more creativity if they allow themselves to be bored occasionally.

Listen & Repeat

🔊
Instructions: Listen carefully to each phrase from the video. Pay attention to the pronunciation, stress, and rhythm. Repeat each phrase out loud, first as a group, then individually.
1
"Boredom makes you more creative, altruistic, introspective…"
Stress the content words: MAKES, MORE, CRE-a-tive, al-TRU-is-tic, in-tro-SPEC-tive
2
"Every time you're waiting for something, you have a decision to make."
Notice the natural rhythm — unstressed small words (you're, for, a) connect to the stressed words
3
"It seems like an insignificant decision."
in-sig-NIF-i-cant — four syllables, stress on the third
4
"You are likely making yourself less creative, less altruistic."
The repeated word "less" creates a list rhythm — keep the same stress and tone for each item
5
"This aimless state seems to cultivate thoughts about what you want to do with your life."
AIM-less · CUL-ti-vate — check the syllable stress on these two key words
6
"So think carefully before pressing that button."
Short, direct sentence — deliver it with a pause after "So"
7
"If your brain is always consumed with other stimuli, you'll rarely ponder the bigger picture."
POND-er = to think deeply about something. Stretch the word "rarely" slightly for emphasis.

Veritasium — "Why Boredom is Good For You" (Adapted for A2/B1)

Boredom makes you more creative, altruistic, introspective, and helps with autobiographical planning. I feel like this video might come across as a little preachy, but the person I am really talking to is myself. I know how useful boredom can be, but I still find myself trying to fill every moment with something to do.

Here is an experiment that might surprise you. Researchers put people in an empty room for just a few minutes. There was nothing in the room — no phone, no book, nothing. But there was a button. And the people knew that pressing this button would give them an electric shock.

What happened? At the end of the experiment, 25% of women and 60% of men chose to shock themselves. They preferred pain to doing nothing. This tells us something important: boredom is so uncomfortable that people will do almost anything to escape it.

But here is the interesting part. When our brains have nothing to do, they don't actually stop working. Instead, they activate what scientists call the default mode network — a special system in the brain. This aimless state seems to cultivate thoughts about what you want to do with your life. You begin to think about your past, your relationships, and your future. This is called autobiographical planning.

Studies show that when people are given boring tasks — tasks that only use a small part of their brain — they often start thinking about the future and making plans. If your brain is always consumed with other stimuli, you will rarely ponder the bigger picture and set long-term goals for yourself.

Boredom also makes us more altruistic. When we are bored, we think more about other people and feel more empathy. And it makes us more creative — new ideas and connections can only happen when the brain has free, open space to explore.

Every time you are waiting for something, you have a decision to make which seems like a tiny one: pull out your phone for a few minutes — or just be bored and experience only your thoughts. It seems insignificant. But by reaching for your phone, you are likely making yourself less creative, less altruistic, less introspective, and less able to plan for your future.

In short, you are the real-world example of someone shocking themselves to avoid boredom. Except in your case, the pain goes much deeper — to the very nature of who you are and who you will become.

So think carefully before pressing that button.
